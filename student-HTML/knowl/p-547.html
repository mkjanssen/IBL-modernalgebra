In this chapter, we come to the heart of the text: a structural investigation of unique factorization in the familiar contexts of \(\Z\) and \(F[x]\text{.}\) In Section 3.1, we explore theorems that formalize much of our understanding of that quintessential high school algebra problem: factoring polynomials. As we saw in Theorem 1.2.8 and Theorem 2.4.18, both \(\Z\) and \(F[x]\) have a division algorithm and, thus, are Euclidean domains. In Section 3.2, we explore the implications for multiplication in Euclidean domains. That is: given that we have a well-behaved division algorithm in an integral domain, what can we say about the factorization properties of the domain?
