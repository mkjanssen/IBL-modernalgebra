Without loss of generality, assume \(p_1 \lt p_2 \lt \cdots \lt p_m\) and \(q_1 \lt q_2 \lt \cdots \lt q_s\text{.}\) Given a \(p_i\text{,}\) we know that \(p_i | q_1^{t_1} q_2^{t_2} \cdots q_s^{t_s}\text{,}\) which implies that \(p_i | q_j\) for some \(j\) by Claim 2 in the proof of Lemma 2.8. Further, Lemma 2.8 implies that \(p_i = q_j\text{,}\) and similarly, given a \(q_j\text{,}\) \(q_j = p_i\) for some \(i\text{.}\) Thus, \(m =s\text{,}\) and by the ordering of the \(p_i\)'s and \(q_j\)'s, we have \(p_i = q_j\text{,}\) \(i = 1, 2, \ldots, m\text{.}\) Therefore,

\begin{equation*} n = p_1^{r_1} p_2^{r_2} \cdots p_m^{r_m} = p_1^{t_1} p_2^{t_2} \cdots p_m^{t_m}\text{.} \end{equation*}
