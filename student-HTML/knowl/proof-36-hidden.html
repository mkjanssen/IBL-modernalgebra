Let \(d\) be a gcd of \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) let \(u\in R^\times\text{,}\) and \(e =ud\text{.}\) We claim \(e\) is also a gcd of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
Since \(d|a\) and \(d|b\) there are \(k_1,k_2\in R\) such that \(d k_1 = a\) and \(d k_2 = b\text{.}\) Then \(e (u^{-1} k_1) = a\) and \(e (u^{-1} k_2) = b\text{,}\) so \(e|a\) and \(e|b\text{.}\)
Let \(f\) be a common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) Since \(d\) is a gcd, \(f|d\text{,}\) i.e., \(f k_3 = d\text{.}\) Then \(f (uk_3) = e\text{,}\) so \(f|e\text{.}\)
Thus, \(e\) is a gcd of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
